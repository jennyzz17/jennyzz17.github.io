Visualization

Setting up a blog for the third assignment.

1 minute read

This blog is for sharing three informative interactive visualization plotted based on dataset at Malaria Data Website. malaria_inc.csv includes data of malaria incidence for country for all ages across the world across time. Meanwhile, malaria_deaths.csv includes data of malaria deaths by country for all age across the world across time. Finally, malaria_deaths_age.csv includes malaria deaths by age across the world and time. For visualization, I used the library of ‚ÄòPlotly‚Äô since it can easily create various interactive graphs. ‚ÄòPlotly‚Äô can also help export the plots into html files, which would save lot of works to embed them on static web. Therefore, I chose ‚ÄòPlotly‚Äô for this assignment.

After cleaning and summarizing the data from malaria_deaths.csv, I filtered out the five countries with the most severe malaria deaths. They are Burundi, Malawi, Mozambique, Sierra Leone, and Uganda. Then for these countries, the trend of the number of deaths along the time was visualized.

Based on the comparison, Uganda, Mozambique, and Malawi all showed a strictly decreasing trend of number of deaths from 1990 to 2015. However, both the numbers of deaths in Burundi and Sierra Leone went up reaching their maximum first in 2004 or 2005, and then went down later. This decreasing trend is reasonable due to the advancement of current treatment and availability of hospital resources. In addition, in 1990, Uganda had the highest number of deaths because of malaria. But in 2015, it ended with the second lowest number of deaths. From this perspective, this phenomena indicated the rapid development of public health in Uganda. Nevertheless, Sierra Leone had the lowest number of deaths in 1990, but it ended with the highest number of deaths. By 2015, the death rate of these most severe countries has approximately decreased to 50 per 100000 people.

By looking into more details of these five countries, let‚Äôs now focus on malaria_deaths_age.csv. After filtering out the above five countries, I created a plot with two drop-down buttons. One of the buttons is for selecting which country among the five is interested. Another button is for selecting the time of year. The default setting is ‚ÄòCountry‚Äô equals to Burundi and ‚ÄòYear‚Äô equals to 1990. Then this bar plot is showing the number of deaths for different age groups for this specific country and this specific time. Changing the ‚ÄòCountry‚Äô or ‚ÄòYear‚Äô button can help to see the changes in distribution of age groups for different countries and time. (Visualization is not available now)

Finally, to visualize malaria_inc.csv, I grouped the countries based on their continents for reflecting the general difference between continents. Moreover, I merged this dataset with malaria_deaths.csv to create a scatterplot seeing the relationship between incidence and deaths. The slider below this scatterplot is for changing the time.

From this plot, the size represents the relative value of deaths. The color indicates different continents. We can see in the year of 2000, the countries with highest incidence and highest deaths are all in Africa. Among the African countries, Ethiopia has the highest incidence but quite low deaths. Meanwhile, Burkina Faso has the second highest incidence and the second highest deaths. Except for African countries, two countries (Solomon Islands and Papua New Guinea) from Oceania also have relative high incidence or deaths. In 2015, all countries had less incidence and less deaths compared with 2000. But African countries still had the highest incidence and deaths compared with other continents.
